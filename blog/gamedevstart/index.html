Starting in Game Development

June 3, 2020

2 minute read

 Twitter

 Facebook

 Reddit

 LinkedIn

 Pinterest

 Email

I’m a man of many ideas. Wanting to develop a simple game, then move onto more complex ones, is one of those ideas. Of course, many have done this before me and many will after me, so I have no qualms about standing on the shoulders of giants, and using a game engine that suits my needs.

Now, onto that. In the game engine world, there are two contenders that everyone knows about (so far as I can tell). Unity and Unreal Engine. There are, of course, many others, but these tend to be pointed to as examples of user-friendly (in the case of Unity), or amazingly good (in the case of Unreal). So I set out to determine which one suits my needs.

After having trialed both of these, I decided that neither are good for me. Both use C#, which is something I’m proficient at, but Unity seems to be far from user-friendly. Indeed, it appears to be a mess of conflicting, and redundant, code that the documentation struggles to contain. Unreal on the other hand, just has a ridiculously steep learning curve that I struggled to even start with.

That’s when I came across Godot. Godot appears to be an up-and-coming engine, gaining new abilities all the time, and has a unique node-structure that makes for incredibly easy inheritance-based coding. It also comes with its own Godot Script, a python-based scripting language that is incredibly easy to learn. Though if you want more control, it now supports C# as well.

It’s open source, a practice that I always enjoy supporting, and has a free-to-use license, meaning you never pay a cent regardless of how successful your game is (of course, all my games will be wildly successful and I’ll be a millionaire by the end of the year).

So begins my learning of Godot, and such. I will make posts as essentially a sort of tutorial as I learn things myself. So, I hope I’m not terrible at it.
