Learning practical machine learning

January 1, 0001 David Ing

4 minute read

 Twitter

 Facebook

 Reddit

 LinkedIn

 Pinterest

 Email

I’ve dabbled a bit in machine learning (ML) before, having taking the introductory course by the famous Andrew Ng on Coursera. However, upon digging a bit deeper, MATLAB is somewhat outdated to use as a basis for ML tasks these days. Additionally, while the course provided a solid foundation for understanding different types of ML learning algorithms, from a more practical point of view it wasn’t as useful. Mostly because the majority of the assignments were mostly pre-filled, leaving the student to create one or two functions that were essentially copy-pasting a piece of math from the lecture slides into MATLAB.

However, I have many grand ideas for ML that require a working understanding. To this end, I am going to undertake the free Fast AI course, as well as read what appears to be an eminent text book Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach. I hope to keep a bit of a guide to my journey here.

So, this is the first post! Straight away, I know this course means business. Why? It requires GPU usage. It has several recommendations, but I’ve chosen to go with Google Colab because it’s free. Unfortunately, the entire course is done in Jupyter Notebooks, which I am not a fan of but I understand the necessity when catering to such a large audience. The course promises to allow you to create world-class models for image classification, text interpretation, prediction, and recommendations after just 7 lessons! Providing you put the work in, of course.

Since the course is available for free, etc, this won’t be a blow by blow account of the lesson. I mean, the lessons are there. Instead, it’s going to be the ideas that I decide are the most important to me.

The first lesson is on Image classification, a typical problem in ML. We’ve all seen the pictures of people trying out ML, and building a classifier that then classifies their cat as a dog. The lesson is really good, with guidance at all steps, a good forum, and encouragement to not necessarily look too deeply into each step at this stage. The focus is on getting a working model, and deeper understanding comes later in Part 2.

The basic structure of the program goes something like Get Data → Process Data → Train Model → Results → Fine Tune → Train Model → Results. This seems like a fairly standard workloop.

An important take away from this lesson was that for GPU’s to function at their peak performance, the datasets need to be the same size. In fact, the lecturer described this is a shortcoming in modern deep learning techniques. A possible avenue for research, perhaps? Probably not for me though, since it would most likely pertain more to GPU architecture than ML algorithms.

He also mentions Transfer Learning, which sounds like a great way to reduce the amount of time spent training your neural net. So for my ideas, perhaps I can cut down on the amount of work I need to do by utilising existing trained sets? Definitely worth looking into.

Anyway, by the end of this first lesson, he’s taken us through the code necessary to train a model to identify breeds of cats and dogs. FastAI is an incredibly powerful piece of technology, given that it accomplishes this in around 5 lines of code.

Now for the exercise: building our own image classification project. So first I need to decide what to classify. I’m thinking if this neural net can classify images of cats and dogs so well that it can actually determine the breed correctly 96% of the time, then it should be able to classify me some clouds.

Actually my wife came to me and suggested I do one to classify the different kinds of bees, or maybe wasps, so that’s a great idea as well. I can also package these up into a simple phone app, so that would be cool.

Since I have two different topics I want to explore, I’ll create separate blog posts for each of them, and come back some time in the future to link them in here.
